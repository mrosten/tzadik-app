Rabbi Moshe Chaim Luzzatto (Ramchal)

Born: 1707, Padua, Republic of Venice (Italy)
Died: 1746, Acre, Ottoman Syria (Israel)

Rabbi Moshe Chaim Luzzatto, also known by the acronym Ramchal, was an Italian Jewish rabbi, kabbalist, and philosopher. He was a prolific writer and is considered one of the founders of modern Hebrew literature.

His most famous work, Mesillat Yesharim ("Path of the Just"), is a classic of Jewish ethical literature that is studied in yeshivas throughout the world. He also wrote numerous works on Kabbalah, including Derech Hashem ("The Way of God"), which is a systematic exposition of Jewish theology. He moved to the Land of Israel in 1743 and died there in a plague.

A Famous Story: The Ramchal and the Maggid

The Ramchal was a very holy man, and it is said that he had a maggid, an angelic being who would visit him and teach him the secrets of the Torah. The maggid would appear to him in the form of a voice, and would speak to him for hours on end.

The maggid's teachings are recorded in the book Maggid Mesharim, which is a collection of the maggid's discourses to the Ramchal. The book is a classic of Kabbalistic literature and is studied by mystics and scholars to this day.

This story illustrates the belief that the Torah is not just a book of laws, but a living and breathing thing, and that there are hidden secrets in the Torah that can only be revealed to those who are worthy. It is a testament to the belief that the study of Torah is a mystical experience, and that it is possible to connect to the divine through the study of the Torah.
